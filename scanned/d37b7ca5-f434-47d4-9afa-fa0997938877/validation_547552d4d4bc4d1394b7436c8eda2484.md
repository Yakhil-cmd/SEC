### Title
`StakeMaturity` Increases Neuron Voting Power Without Refreshing `voting_power_refreshed_timestamp_seconds`, Causing Ballot Voting Power to Diverge from Actual Stake - (File: `rs/nns/governance/src/governance.rs`)

---

### Summary

In the NNS Governance canister, the `stake_maturity_of_neuron` function increases a neuron's `staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent` (which directly contributes to `stake_e8s()` and therefore to voting power) without updating `voting_power_refreshed_timestamp_seconds`. Because `deciding_voting_power` is computed as `potential_voting_power * adjustment_factor(time_since_last_refreshed)`, a neuron that has not refreshed recently will have its newly staked maturity counted in `potential_voting_power` but penalized (or zeroed) in `deciding_voting_power` â€” the value actually placed in proposal ballots. This is the IC analog of the Berachain bug: a state-changing operation (stake increase) is applied, but the "update" that would make the new stake fully reflected in governance (the refresh timestamp) is not sent, causing the voting power used in ballots to diverge from the underlying stake.

---

### Finding Description

The `stake_maturity_of_neuron` function in `rs/nns/governance/src/governance.rs` (lines 2753â€“2843) moves tokens from `maturity_e8s_equivalent` into `staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent`: [1](#0-0) 

`staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent` is included in `stake_e8s()` (via `neuron_stake_e8s`), which is the base of `potential_voting_power`: [2](#0-1) 

However, `deciding_voting_power` â€” the value actually written into proposal ballots â€” is computed by multiplying `potential_voting_power` by an `adjustment_factor` that decays linearly to zero based on `time_since_last_refreshed`: [3](#0-2) 

`stake_maturity_of_neuron` does **not** call `neuron.refresh_voting_power(now_seconds)` after updating `staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent`. Compare this to `make_proposal` and `register_vote`, which both explicitly call `refresh_voting_power` after their state changes: [4](#0-3) [5](#0-4) 

When `compute_ballots_for_standard_proposal` is called at proposal creation time, it iterates all active neurons and uses `deciding_voting_power` for each ballot: [6](#0-5) 

A neuron that staked maturity but has not refreshed recently will have its new stake counted in `potential_voting_power` (used for spike detection) but penalized or zeroed in `deciding_voting_power` (used in ballots). This creates an ordering inconsistency: the stake increase is applied, but the governance "acknowledgment" of that increase (the refresh) is not.

---

### Impact Explanation

**Governance authorization bug / incorrect voting power in ballots.**

- A neuron controller who calls `StakeMaturity` to increase their neuron's stake will find that the increased stake does **not** proportionally increase their `deciding_voting_power` in subsequent proposal ballots, if their `voting_power_refreshed_timestamp_seconds` is stale (> 6 months old).
- Conversely, a neuron that is near the staleness threshold can stake maturity to inflate `potential_voting_power` (used in spike detection) without a corresponding increase in `deciding_voting_power`, potentially manipulating the spike-detection mechanism: if `potential_voting_power` is pushed above 1.5Ă— the minimum snapshot, the spike-detection fallback to a previous snapshot is triggered, which may use even older (lower) voting power values for all neurons â€” benefiting the attacker by suppressing other neurons' deciding power. [7](#0-6) 

---

### Likelihood Explanation

**Medium.** Any NNS neuron controller can call `manage_neuron` with `StakeMaturity` at any time â€” this is an unprivileged, externally reachable ingress call. The effect is most pronounced for neurons that have not refreshed in 6â€“7 months. The spike-detection manipulation path requires coordinating the timing of `StakeMaturity` calls relative to proposal creation, which is feasible for a motivated attacker with a large neuron.

---

### Recommendation

In `stake_maturity_of_neuron`, after updating `staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent`, call `neuron.refresh_voting_power(now)` so that the increased stake is immediately reflected in `deciding_voting_power` for subsequent proposals. This mirrors the pattern already used in `make_proposal` and `register_vote`. [8](#0-7) 

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Neuron A has `cached_neuron_stake_e8s = 1_000_000`, `maturity_e8s_equivalent = 5_000_000`, `staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent = 0`, and `voting_power_refreshed_timestamp_seconds` set 7 months ago (past the 6-month threshold).
2. Neuron A calls `manage_neuron` â†’ `StakeMaturity { percentage_to_stake: 100 }`.
3. `stake_maturity_of_neuron` sets `staked_maturity_e8s_equivalent = 5_000_000`, increasing `stake_e8s()` from 1M to 6M. `voting_power_refreshed_timestamp_seconds` is **not** updated.
4. A proposal is created. `compute_ballots_for_standard_proposal` calls `potential_and_deciding_voting_power` for Neuron A.
5. `potential_voting_power` reflects the new 6M stake. But `adjustment_factor` is computed from `time_since_last_refreshed = 7 months`, which is past `start_reducing_voting_power_after_seconds` (6 months), so `deciding_voting_power` is reduced (or zero if past `clear_following_after_seconds`).
6. Neuron A's ballot is assigned a `deciding_voting_power` far below what its actual stake warrants â€” the stake increase from `StakeMaturity` is silently ignored in governance outcomes.
7. Meanwhile, `potential_voting_power` (used for spike detection) reflects the full 6M, potentially triggering the spike-detection fallback and suppressing all other neurons' deciding power in the proposal. [9](#0-8) [7](#0-6)

        };

        let deciding_voting_power = adjustment_factor * potential_voting_power.floor();
