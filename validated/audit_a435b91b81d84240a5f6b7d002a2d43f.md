### Title
NEAR Validator Can Manipulate EVM Randomness via Known Per-Block `random_seed` — (`engine-precompiles/src/random.rs`, `engine/src/engine.rs`)

---

### Summary

The Aurora Engine exposes NEAR's per-block VRF seed to EVM contracts through two surfaces: the `RandomSeed` precompile at `0xc104f4840573bed437190daf5d2898c2bdf928ac` and the EVM `PREVRANDAO`/`block.difficulty` opcode (via `block_randomness()`). Both return the same value: the NEAR block's `random_seed`, which is known to the NEAR chunk producer **before** they finalize the block. A NEAR validator can exploit this foreknowledge to selectively include or exclude their own EVM transactions, biasing randomness-dependent outcomes in their favor and stealing funds from other users of Aurora EVM contracts.

---

### Finding Description

**Surface 1 — `RandomSeed` precompile:**

The precompile at `engine-precompiles/src/random.rs` returns `self.random_seed` directly, and its own documentation states:

> "It will return the same seed if called multiple time in the same block." [1](#0-0) 

This seed is sourced from NEAR's `random_seed` host function: [2](#0-1) 

**Surface 2 — EVM `PREVRANDAO` opcode:**

`block_randomness()` in the engine's EVM handler also returns `self.env.random_seed()`, meaning Solidity contracts using `block.prevrandao` or `block.difficulty` receive the same NEAR per-block seed: [3](#0-2) 

**Root cause — NEAR VRF seed is known to the chunk producer before block finalization:**

In NEAR Protocol, the `random_seed` for a block is computed as `VRF(validator_private_key, prev_block_hash)`. The chunk producer who is scheduled to produce a block can compute this value before they publish the block. They therefore know the exact value that will be returned by both the `RandomSeed` precompile and `PREVRANDAO` for every transaction they choose to include in that block.

The `BlockMetadata` struct confirms the seed is a single per-block value: [4](#0-3) 

---

### Impact Explanation

Any Aurora EVM contract that uses the `RandomSeed` precompile or `block.prevrandao`/`block.difficulty` to determine value distribution (lotteries, NFT trait assignment, gambling contracts, random winner selection) is vulnerable. A NEAR validator who is also a participant in such a contract can:

1. Compute the `random_seed` for the block they are scheduled to produce.
2. Determine the outcome of the randomness-dependent contract call for their own address.
3. If the outcome is favorable, include their transaction in the block and collect the prize.
4. If the outcome is unfavorable, withhold their transaction (skip or delay it) and try again in a future block where they are the producer.

This constitutes **direct theft of user funds** held in Aurora EVM contracts. The attacker wins at the expense of other honest participants who deposited funds expecting fair randomness.

**Impact: Critical — direct theft of user funds at-rest in Aurora EVM contracts.**

---

### Likelihood Explanation

Becoming a NEAR chunk producer requires staking NEAR tokens, which is a permissionless, economically accessible action. The original Entropy.sol report's exploit scenario explicitly mirrors this: "Alice additionally stakes enough Ether to become a validator." The same applies here. The attack requires no privileged access, no leaked keys, and no social engineering — only a staking deposit and patience to wait for a scheduled block production slot.

**Likelihood: Medium** — requires validator stake, but the attack is fully permissionless and the profit from a single successful manipulation of a high-value contract can far exceed the cost of staking.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Short term:** Document clearly that the `RandomSeed` precompile and `block.prevrandao` are **not safe for use as the sole source of randomness in value-bearing contracts**. Publish guidance on Aurora's developer documentation explaining the NEAR validator foreknowledge property.

2. **Long term:** Consider a commit-reveal scheme at the Aurora contract layer, or integrate a verifiable delay function (VDF) or threshold VRF so that no single NEAR validator can predict the seed used for a given EVM transaction. Alternatively, mix the per-block seed with transaction-specific data (e.g., the action hash) before exposing it to EVM contracts, so that the validator cannot predict the per-transaction outcome even if they know the block seed.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Alice stakes NEAR tokens and becomes a chunk producer on the NEAR network.
2. A lottery contract is deployed on Aurora EVM. It accepts ETH deposits from users and uses `block.prevrandao` (or calls the `RandomSeed` precompile at `0xc104f4840573bed437190daf5d2898c2bdf928ac`) to pick a winner at the end of each round.
3. Alice deposits into the lottery contract.
4. When Alice is scheduled to produce a NEAR chunk, she computes `VRF(her_private_key, prev_block_hash)` to obtain the `random_seed` for her block.
5. She simulates the lottery contract's winner-selection logic off-chain using the known seed.
6. If the simulation shows she wins: she includes the lottery resolution transaction in her block and collects all deposited funds.
7. If the simulation shows she loses: she does not include the resolution transaction, waits for her next scheduled production slot, and repeats from step 4.
8. Honest depositors' funds are drained.

The entry path is fully reachable by an unprivileged EVM user who also holds NEAR validator stake. The vulnerable code path is:

- `engine-sdk/src/near_runtime.rs` `random_seed()` reads NEAR host `random_seed` → [2](#0-1) 
- `engine/src/engine.rs` `block_randomness()` exposes it as `PREVRANDAO` → [3](#0-2) 
- `engine-precompiles/src/random.rs` `run()` returns it directly to EVM callers → [5](#0-4)
